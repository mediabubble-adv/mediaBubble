# Religious Context — Muslim & Christian Egypt

## Overview

Egypt is ~90% Muslim and ~10% Christian (predominantly Coptic Orthodox). Religious identity is central to most Egyptians' daily lives. Content that ignores or mishandles religious context will be rejected by the audience.

---

## Islamic Context

### Key Religious Periods

| Period | 2025 Dates | Content Considerations |
|--------|------------|----------------------|
| Ramadan | March 1–29 | Prime content period. Focus on spirituality, family, charity, health during fasting. Peak engagement 9 PM–2 AM. |
| Eid El-Fitr | March 30–April 2 | Family celebrations, post-Ramadan health transitions, gift-giving |
| Hajj | June 5–10 | Spiritual content, health tips for pilgrims |
| Eid El-Adha | June 10–13 | Family gatherings, sacrifice (animal), charity themes |
| Islamic New Year | June 27 | Reflection, goal-setting content |
| Ashura | July 6 | Fasting day, spiritual reflection |
| Mawlid Al-Nabawi | September 5 | Prophet Muhammad's birthday — spiritual, community themes |
| Israa wal-Mi'raj | January 27 | Spiritual content (less commercial) |

### Content Guidelines for Muslim Periods

**Ramadan — Critical Content Opportunity**
- Health content during fasting is highly relevant (diabetes, hydration, nutrition)
- Best engagement period of the year for Egyptian audiences
- Evening hours (after iftar) are peak — schedule posts for 9 PM–12 AM
- Charity and community themes resonate
- Sadaka (صدقة) framing works well for healthcare access content
- Avoid: Food-heavy content during fasting hours; scheduling posts during prayer times

**Hajj Content**
- Health preparation for pilgrims (vaccinations, physical readiness)
- Trusted medical services in Mecca/Medina context
- Avoid: Over-commercialization of a spiritual journey

### Islamic References in Content

| Reference | Appropriate Use | Avoid |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| إن شاء الله | Future plans, hopes, health outcomes | Overuse in transactional content |
| الحمد لله | Positive outcomes, thanksgiving | After bad news |
| بسم الله | Beginnings, procedures, meals | Superficial use |
| ماشاء الله | Compliments, admiration | Tempting fate (some believe it invites envy) |
| ربنا | God in personal, warm context | Reducing God to marketing tool |
| سنة | Prophetic tradition / practice | Misrepresenting religious practice |

## Coptic Christian Context

### Key Religious Periods

| Period | Typical Dates | Content Considerations |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| Coptic Christmas | January 7 | Respectful greetings, family themes |
| Epiphany | January 19 | Water-related symbolism (baptism) |
| Great Lent | 55 days before Easter | Fasting period — fish consumption increases |
| Coptic Easter | April 20 (2025) | Major holiday, family gatherings |
| Nayrouz (Coptic New Year) | September 11 | Cultural content |
| Feast of the Cross | September 27 | Cultural content |

### Content Guidelines for Christian Periods
- Include Coptic holiday greetings alongside Islamic ones in national content
- Avoid scheduling major campaigns on Coptic holidays (engagement drops)
- Coptic fasting involves vegan diet — relevant for nutrition content
- Coptic Christmas (Jan 7) and Easter are family gathering occasions — health content opportunity

### Inclusive Religious Language

| Inclusive Phrase | Context |
|------------------|---------|
| كل سنة وانت طيب | Eid/holiday greeting (works for both Muslims and Christians — and now used for secular occasions too) |
| أعيادكم مباركة | Holiday blessings (inclusive) |
| ربنا معاك / معاكي | God be with you (neutral — understood by both) |
| في ميزان حسناتك | May it be in your good deeds (neutral — accepted by both) |

### What to Avoid
- Exclusive Islamic-only greetings in national content (use inclusive alternatives)
- Ignoring Coptic holidays in scheduling
- Religious imagery that only represents one faith
- Assuming all Egyptians fast Ramadan (Christians don't)

## Modesty Guidelines

### Visual Content
- Women: Modest dress expected in healthcare contexts (hijab not required but respectful dress is)
- Men: Shirtless imagery is inappropriate for healthcare advertising (context exceptions: medical)
- Couples: Physical affection in imagery should be minimal and tasteful
- Medical imagery: Educational nudity acceptable with clear context and disclaimers

### Language
- Avoid sexual references even in clinical health content (use euphemisms)
- Reproductive health: Use scientific Arabic terms, not slang
- Mental health: Frame as راحة نفسية (psychological comfort), not "mental illness"

### Content by Conservatism Level

| Region | Modesty Sensitivity |
|--------|-------------------|
| Cairo (modern areas) | Moderate |
| Alexandria | Moderate |
| Delta | Moderate-High |
| Upper Egypt (Sa'eed) | High |
| Red Sea / Tourism | Low-Moderate |

## Religious Authority in Healthcare

### Using Religious References for Health Content

| Topic | Effective Framing |
|-------|-------------------|
| Preventive care | "جسمك أمانة من ربنا — حافظ عليه" |
| Mental health | "راحة النفس من راحة القلب — والقلب بيذكر ربنا" |
| Smoking cessation | "جسمك مش ملكك انت بس — ربنا ائتمنك عليه" |
| Exercise | "العقل السليم في الجسم السليم — وأكيد ربنا بيحب القوي" |
| Diet | "كلوا واشربوا ولا تسرفوا" (Quran 7:31 — direct religious reference) |
| Elder care | "وأوصينا الإنسان بوالديه" (Quran — family duty to care for parents) |

### Important
- Only use Quranic verses and Hadith when they are genuinely relevant
- Don't fabricate religious references for marketing effect
- Consult a religious authority if uncertain about a reference
- Christian audiences may not connect with Islamic verses — use universal values instead
