# Business Etiquette — Egyptian Professional Culture

## Relationship Building (العلاقات)

In Egypt, business is personal. Relationships precede transactions.

### Key Principles
- **Trust first, business second** — Egyptians need to know and trust you before doing business
- **Face-to-face matters** — Digital relationships supplement, not replace, in-person meetings
- **Wasta (واسطة)** — Connections and referrals are essential, not optional. It's not "corruption" in the Egyptian context — it's how trust is transmitted
- **Time investment** — First meetings are about relationship, not agenda. Expect tea/coffee and conversation before business
- **"معرفش" is rare** — Egyptians rarely say "I don't know" directly (face-saving). Ask differently or follow up

### Building Trust Sequence
1. **Introduction** — Through a trusted mutual contact (most effective)
2. **First meeting** — Social conversation, shared context, personal connection
3. **Follow-up** — Demonstrate reliability through actions, not words
4. **Small commitment** — Start with a small, low-risk collaboration
5. **Repeat** — Consistency builds the relationship over time

### Content Implications for Business Communications
- Use "صديقنا" / "زميلنا" when referring to partners (warmth)
- Reference previous interactions — "زي ما اتفقنا", "كنا بنتكلم في"
- Showcase team members and their personalities (not just credentials)
- Include testimonials from known industry figures
- Don't rush to "the deal" — build value first

## Negotiation Style

### Characteristics
| Aspect | Egyptian Style | Western Expectation | Adaptation |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|------------|
| Pace | Slow, relationship-first | Fast, transaction-first | Take your time; don't rush |
| Directness | Indirect, face-saving | Direct, transparent | Read between the lines; "لا" may come as "إن شاء الله" |
| Price negotiation | Expected, even when fixed | Price is usually firm | Build room for negotiation or offer value-adds |
| Decision making | Hierarchical, consensus-based | Individual authority | Identify the actual decision-maker (may not be who you're meeting) |
| Commitment | Verbal commitment is binding | Written contract is binding | Follow up verbal agreements in writing respectfully |

### Understanding "إن شاء الله" in Business
In Egyptian business context, "إن شاء الله" can mean:
1. "Yes, I will do this" (literal — God willing)
2. "I'll try but no promises" (polite hedge)
3. "No, but I won't say no directly" (face-saving decline)

**How to tell:** If followed by specific next steps = meaning 1. If vague = likely meaning 2 or 3. Follow up clearly.

### Price Negotiation Framework
- Negotiation is expected — don't give best price first
- Frame discounts as relationship gestures, not price cuts
- Value-adds (free consultation, extended support) are often better than price reductions
- Use "إحترام" (respect) framing: "احتراماً لعلاقتنا"

## Meetings & Communication

### Meeting Structure
1. **Greeting** (2-5 min) — Warmth, personal inquiry (how's family, how's health)
2. **Social context** (5-10 min) — Current events, shared interests
3. **Business context** — Why we're meeting, shared goals
4. **Proposal** — What we're suggesting
5. **Discussion** — Questions, clarifications, negotiation
6. **Conclusion** — Summary, next steps, future meeting
7. **Farewell** — Warm goodbye, invitation for future connection

### Timing & Punctuality
- **Flexible** — Being 15-30 minutes late is normal for social meetings
- **Formal meetings** — Be on time yourself, but don't be offended if others are late
- **Ramadan hours** — Business hours shift dramatically (9 AM-2 PM, then 9 PM-midnight)
- **Friday** — Officially off for many businesses (prayer + family day)
- **Sunday-Thursday** — Standard work week
- **Summer hours** — Many businesses shorten hours June-August

### Preferred Communication Channels

| Channel | Use Case | Response Time |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| WhatsApp | Most common for all business communication | Minutes to hours |
| Phone call | Urgent matters, relationship building | Immediate |
| Email | Formal communication, documentation | 1-3 days |
| LinkedIn | Professional networking, B2B | Days to weeks |
| In-person | Important meetings, relationship building | Scheduled |

**Important:** WhatsApp is not "too casual" for business in Egypt — it IS business communication. Don't insist on email-only with Egyptian partners.

## Hierarchy in Business

Egyptian organizations are hierarchical. Decision-making flows top-down.

### Content Implications for B2B
- Always address the most senior person first
- Copy more junior team members after senior approval
- Show deference to titles and positions in all communications
- Don't bypass hierarchy (going over someone's head is a serious breach)
- Decision-makers are rarely the first person you meet

### Working with Different Levels

| Level | Communication Style | Decision Authority |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Executive / Owner | Formal, respectful, direct | Full authority |
| Senior management | Professional, relationship-based | High authority, may need approval |
| Middle management | Warm, collaborative | Limited authority, can influence |
| Junior staff | Supportive, encouraging | No decision authority |

## Business Gift-Giving & Hospitality

### Appropriate Gifts
- Small, thoughtful gifts are appreciated
- Avoid alcohol (even for Muslim partners, it's inappropriate in business)
- Dates, chocolates, high-quality food items are safe
- Business-related gifts (quality pen set, notebook) are professional
- Flowers for occasions (but not for male partners — chocolates instead)

### Hospitality Rules
- Always accept tea/coffee when offered (refusing is slightly rude)
- Don't start business until hospitality is served
- Compliment the hospitality
- Host is expected to insist on paying — insist once, then accept graciously

## Taboos in Business Communication

| Topic | Why Avoid |
|-------|-----------|
| Politics | Highly divisive, can end relationships |
| Religion (beyond greetings) | Can offend if not aligned |
| Personal wealth / salary | Inappropriate |
| Family problems | Too personal for business context |
| Criticism of Egypt | Perceived as insulting national pride |
| Criticism of partner's competitors directly | Bad form — focus on YOUR strengths |
| Sexual or gender-related humor | Never appropriate |
| Corruption or "under the table" references | Serious insult to integrity |
