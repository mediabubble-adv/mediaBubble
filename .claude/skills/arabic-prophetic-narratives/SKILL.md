# arabic-prophetic-narratives — Qisas al-Anbiya' (Prophetic Narratives)

## Overview

Qisas al-Anbiya' (قصص الأنبياء, "Stories of the Prophets") is the body of Islamic literature and Quranic narratives concerning the prophets before Muhammad. The Quran mentions 25 prophets by name, while hadith and tafsir add details. These narratives are presented not merely as history but as moral exempla — lessons in faith, patience, reliance on God, and the consequences of disbelief.

## Key Comparative Narrative Analysis

### Abraham (Ibrahim) — The Hanif Paradigm

| Source | Account | Distinctive Element |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| Quran | Idol-breaking, fire miracle, sacrifice of Ishmael | Emphasis on pure monotheism (hanifiyya); destroy idols; son not specified |
| Bible (Genesis) | Ur → Haran → Canaan; sacrifice of Isaac | Different route; son named Isaac |
| Islamic consensus | Ishmael was the intended son; Abraham restored Ka'bah | Son Ishmael; Ka'bah-centered |

### Moses (Musa) — Liberation Paradigm

| Theme | Quranic Treatment | Biblical Treatment |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Infancy | Box in Nile; taken by Pharaoh's wife (not daughter) | Basket in Nile; taken by daughter |
| Exodus | Focus on signs (9 plagues), Pharaoh's hardening vs signs | 10 plagues; Passover; extended narrative |
| Khidr (18:65–82) | Unique to Quran — no parallel in Bible | A lesson in divine wisdom vs human judgment |

### Jesus ('Isa) — Islamic Christology

| Doctrine | Islamic Position | Christian Position |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Birth | Virgin birth (Maryam), miracle | Virgin birth, fully divine and fully human |
| Nature | Prophet, Messiah, Word of God (kalimat Allah), but NOT divine | Second person of the Trinity, fully divine |
| Crucifixion | Not killed/crucified; it was made to appear so (4:157) | Crucified, died, resurrected |
| Return | Will return before Judgment Day to defeat Dajjal (Antichrist) | Second Coming at the End of Times |
| Miracles | By God's permission: healing, raising dead, clay birds | Own divine authority: healing, exorcism, resurrection |

## Methodological Principles

### Dealing with Isra'iliyyat

Isra'iliyyat (إسرائيليات) refers to narratives from Jewish and Christian sources incorporated into early Islamic tafsir and qisas literature:

| Category | Assessment | How to Handle |
|----------|------------|---------------|
| Confirmed by Quran/Sunnah | Acceptable | Reference as supporting material |
| Contradicts Quran/Sunnah | Rejected | Discard explicitly |
| Neither confirmed nor rejected | Suspended (tawaqquf) | May be reported with caveat: "الله أعلم" (God knows best) |
| Contains absurdities/anthro. | Rejected | Harmful; do not propagate |

### Moral & Theological Themes

Each prophetic narrative carries specific lessons:

| Theme | Example Prophet | Qur'anic Emphasis |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| Patience (Sabr) | Ayyub, Ya'qub | 38:44, 12:86 |
| Trust (Tawakkul) | Ibrahim (fire), Musa (Nile) | 29:17, 20:39 |
| Repentance (Tawbah) | Yunus, Adam, Dawud | 21:87, 2:37, 38:24 |
| Perseverance in da'wah | Nuh (950 years), Muhammad (13 years Meccan) | 29:14, 93:3 |
| Rejection of compromise | Shu'ayb (honest trade), Musa (Pharaoh) | 26:181–183, 20:60–73 |
| Forgiveness | Yusuf (brothers), Muhammad (Quraysh) | 12:92, 15:85 |
| Divine justice | Hud ('Ad), Salih (Thamud), Lut (Sodom) | 11:67, 7:78, 11:82 |
