# arabic-art — Arabic Art (Traditional & Contemporary)

## Overview

Arabic art (الفن العربي) spans 14 centuries of visual culture across three continents. It encompasses the sacred arts of Islam (Quranic illumination, mosque architecture) and the secular arts of the Arab world (courtly arts, portraiture, contemporary galleries). This skill covers both the classical Islamic decorative arts tradition and the dynamic modern/contemporary Arab art scene.

## Traditional Arabic/Islamic Arts

### Islamic Geometric Patterns

The three core principles:

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Tessellation (التبليط) | Infinite repeating patterns based on polygons |
| Symmetry (التناظر) | All classical Islamic symmetries (p4m, p6m, cmm, etc.) — mathematical |
| Proportional system (النسبة) | Circle-based construction; every polygon inscribed in a circle |

**Construction families by symmetry:**

| Family | Base Polygon | Example Applications |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| 4-point | Square | Seljuk brickwork, Moroccan zellij, North African woodcarving |
| 6-point | Equilateral triangle/hexagon | Most common; Umayyad, Mamluk, Ottoman |
| 8-point | Two squares rotated 45° | Alhambra, Madrassa Ben Youssef (Marrakech) |
| 10-point | Pentagon/decagon | Muqarnas vaulting, Ilkhanid/Persian |
| 12-point | Dodecagon | Mamluk (Cairo), Al-Mustansiriyya (Baghdad) |
| 16-point | Extended compass construction | Ottoman imperial mosques |

**Techniques:**
- **Girih (گره / tie-knot)**: Persianate geometric mode using strapwork lines; predominantly 10- and 12-point system
- **Zellij (الزليج / cut-tile mosaic)**: Moroccan geometric tilework; individual hand-cut glazed pieces
- **Ab'laq (الأبلق / alternating light/dark masonry)**: Syrian/Egyptian stone architectural geometry

### Arabesque (Tawriq / Tadwin)

The vegetal arabesque (التوريق / التذويب) is the non-geometric decorative counterpart:

| Period | Style | Characteristics |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| Umayyad (661–750) | Naturalistic | Grapevines, acanthus leaves, classical Mediterranean influence |
| Abbasid (750–1258) | Stylized | Samarra stucco: beveled style (شطبة) with abstracted vegetation |
| Fatimid (909–1171) | Figurative + vegetal | Lively human/animal figures within arabesque frames |
| Mamluk (1250–1517) | Architectural arabesque | Stone-carved, deeply three-dimensional |
| Ottoman (1299–1922) | Saz yolu / naturalist | Tulips, carnations, hyacinths — more naturalistic vegetation |
| Safavid (1501–1736) | Elegant, miniature-integrated | Arabesque frames for miniature painting |

### Manuscript Illumination (Tadhib / التذهيب)

The art of gilding and illuminating Quranic and literary manuscripts. A highly specialized craft:

| Component | Arabic | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Headpiece | عنوان / لوحة الافتتاح | Opening pages of Qur'an; rectangular or arched |
| Finispiece | خاتمة | Closing design; often matching headpiece |
| Verse markers | علامات الآيات | Gold rosettes/diamonds with colored dots |
| Juz' markers | علامات الأجزاء | Marginal notations for each thirtieth |
| Sajdah markers | علامات السجدة | Marginal marks indicating prostration verses |
| Hizb markers | علامات الأحزاب | Marginal markers for each half-juz' |
| Binding | التجليد | Tooled leather with geometric central medallion |

Traditional materials:
- **Pigments**: Lapis lazuli (blue), azurite, vermilion (red), orpiment (yellow), malachite (green), white lead
- **Gold**: 23–24k leaf; sometimes shell gold for fine detail
- **Paper**: Handmade, sized, burnished with agate stone

## Contemporary Arab Art Movements

### Hurufiyya (الحروفية / Letter-based Abstraction)

The most significant 20th-century Arab art movement — fusing Arabic calligraphy with modern abstract art:

| Wave | Dates | Key Figures | Characteristics |
|------|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| Pioneers | 1945–1965 | Shaker Hassan al-Said (Iraq), Madiha Omar (Iraq) | Calligraphy as abstract form; spiritual content |
| Institutional | 1965–1985 | Jawad Salim (Iraq), Mahmoud Hamad (Syria) | Ba'athist-era institutional support; nationalist identity |
| Global | 1985–present | Dia al-Azzawi (Iraq), Abdul Qader al-Rais (UAE) | International exhibitions; diasporic production |

**Key contemporary Arab artists (global reputation):**
- **Dia al-Azzawi** (Iraq, b. 1939): Colorful Hurufiyya; Mesopotamian themes; political commentary
- **Mona Hatoum** (Palestine/Lebanon/UK, b. 1952): Conceptual/sculpture; diaspora identity; body and surveillance
- **Shirin Neshat** (Iran/USA, b. 1957): Video/photography; gender and Islamic identity
- **Hassan Hajjaj** (Morocco/UK, b. 1961): Photography/pop; Moroccan identity and global consumerism
- **Wafaa Bilal** (Iraq/USA, b. 1966): New media/performance; war trauma, surveillance, resistance
- **Ahmed Mater** (Saudi Arabia, b. 1979): Conceptual; Saudi transformation, social commentary
- **Etel Adnan** (Lebanon/USA, 1925–2021): Abstract painting; philosopher-poet

## Contemporary Illustration & Visual Arts

### Contemporary Illustration for Arabic Contexts

Arabic illustration today sits at the intersection of classical tradition and global visual language:

| Style | Description | When to Use |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Geometric-contemporary** | Modern reinterpretation of Islamic geometric systems | Brand identity, editorial, cultural institutions |
| **Character illustration** | Arabic-featured characters with cultural specificity | Children's books, social media, apps |
| **Flat arabesque** | Simplified floral/vegetal patterns in contemporary palette | Digital marketing, UI decoration |
| **Calligraphic illustration** | Typography-as-illustration, text integrated into image | Posters, social posts, identity |
| **Hyperrealism** | Photo-realistic portraiture with cultural context | Premium editorial, advertising |

### Cultural Authenticity in Illustration

**Authentic:** Features that reflect lived Arab experience — architecture, clothing, food, gestures, social settings
**Stereotyping:** Camels, deserts, and turbans as default visual shorthand for "Arab" — unless actually contextually accurate

Checklist before finalizing any Arab-world illustration:
- [ ] Does clothing reflect the actual target country/region (not generic "Middle Eastern")?
- [ ] Do architectural backgrounds match the target city/country?
- [ ] Is skin tone diversity represented within the Arab population?
- [ ] Are gender representation choices intentional and appropriate for context?
- [ ] Have living Arab illustrators been consulted or referenced?

### Agent Behavior Guidelines (Visual Arts)

- **Explain cultural context before technique** — why a visual approach matters before how to execute it
- **Distinguish authentic from stereotypical** — actively flag when an illustration choice relies on clichés
- **Reference living Arab artists** — show contemporary practitioners, not just classical examples
- **Note regional differences** — Egyptian aesthetic ≠ Gulf aesthetic ≠ Levantine aesthetic
- **Explain geometric math** — when discussing Islamic geometric patterns, explain the construction principles, not just the visual result
- **Cite museum sources** — use credible institutional references for historical claims
- **Flag appropriation risks** — warn when a design approach may be perceived as extractive or superficial
