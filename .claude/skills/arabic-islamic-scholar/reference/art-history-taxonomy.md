# Art History Taxonomy — Islamic & Arab Art

## Islamic Geometric Design

### Symmetry Families

#### 4-Fold (Square) Symmetry
- Based on square grid
- Primary angle: 90°
- Common in: Ottoman tilework, Mughal architecture
- Construction: compass + square grid
- Examples: Topkapi patterns, Seljuk brickwork

#### 6-Fold (Hexagonal) Symmetry
- Based on hexagonal grid
- Primary angle: 60°
- Common in: Persian tilework, Moroccan zellij
- Construction: compass-only (circle division)
- Examples: Alhambra patterns, Isfahan mosques

#### 8-Fold (Octagonal) Symmetry
- Based on octagon + square
- Primary angles: 45°, 90°
- Most common Islamic symmetry
- Construction: square inscribed in circle, bisected
- Examples: Fatimid woodwork, Mamluk stone carving

#### 10-Fold (Decagonal) Symmetry
- Based on pentagon/decagon
- Primary angle: 36°
- Construction: more complex, requires golden ratio
- Examples: Topkapi Scroll patterns, Timurid architecture

#### 12-Fold (Dodecagonal) Symmetry
- Based on hexagon + triangle
- Primary angle: 30°
- Construction: combination of 3-fold and 4-fold
- Examples: Ottoman muqarnas, Mughal jaali screens

#### 16-Fold Symmetry
- Based on 8-fold subdivision
- Primary angle: 22.5°
- Most complex common symmetry
- Examples: Late Ottoman decoration, Safavid textiles

### Girih Tile System
Five standard tiles used in medieval Islamic architecture:
1. **Decagon** — 10-sided, internal angle 144°
2. **Pentagon** — 5-sided, internal angle 108°
3. **Hexagon** — 6-sided, internal angle 120°
4. **Bowtie** — concave hexagon
5. **Rhombus** — diamond shape

These tiles can generate quasi-crystalline patterns (discovered by Western mathematicians in 2007, used by Islamic craftsmen since the 13th century).

### Zellij (Moroccan Mosaic)
- Cut glazed tile mosaic
- Geometric patterns assembled by hand
- Colors: cobalt blue, emerald green, saffron yellow, white, black
- Centers: Fez, Meknes, Marrakech
- Applications: walls, floors, fountains, mihrabs

## Arabesque Stylization

### Dynastic Evolution

| Dynasty | Period | Characteristics | Media |
|---------|--------|----------------|-------|
| Umayyad | 661–750 | Byzantine mosaic influence, naturalistic vegetal motifs | Mosaic, fresco |
| Abbasid | 750–1258 | Beveled style (Style I, II, III), abstract vegetal | Stucco, wood |
| Fatimid | 909–1171 | Refined carving, figural elements, lusterware | Wood, stone, ceramic |
| Seljuk | 1037–1194 | Interlacing, deep carving, muqarnas | Stone, brick, stucco |
| Ayyubid | 1171–1260 | Simplified, architectural integration | Stone |
| Mamluk | 1250–1517 | Complex interlace, calligraphic integration | Stone, wood, metal |
| Ottoman | 1299–1922 | Iznik floral (tulip, carnation, rose), saz style | Ceramic, textile, manuscript |
| Safavid | 1501–1736 | Elaborate floral, carpet design integration | Carpet, manuscript, tile |
| Mughal | 1526–1857 | Naturalistic floral, pietra dura | Stone inlay, textile |

### Arabesque Principles
- Infinite extension (no beginning or end — reflects infinity of Allah)
- Rhythm and repetition
- Interlacing and overlapping
- Abstraction from natural forms
- Integration with calligraphy and geometry

## Manuscript Illumination (Tadhib)

### Gold Leaf Application
- Burnished gold on prepared surface
- Size: animal glue or gum arabic
- Tooling: agate burnisher
- Layers: underpainting, gold, overpainting

### Pigment Preparation
| Color | Source | Preparation |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Blue | Lapis lazuli (Afghanistan) | Grinding, levigation |
| Green | Malachite | Grinding |
| Red | Cinnabar/vermilion | Grinding, purification |
| White | Lead white | Corrosion process |
| Black | Lamp black | Soot collection |
| Yellow | Orpiment/saffron | Grinding or extraction |
| Gold | Gold leaf | Beating, burnishing |

### Quranic Headpieces
- Surah opening decorations
- Geometric or vegetal frames
- Gold and color
- Vary by region and period

### Verse Markers
- Decorative markers at verse endings
- Numerical indicators
- Juz' markers
- Hizb and rub' markers

## Contemporary Arab Art

### Hurufiyya Movement
Art movement using Arabic letterforms as abstract visual elements.

**Key Figures:**
- Dia al-Azzawi (Iraq, b. 1939) — painting, sculpture
- Hassan Massoudy (Iraq/France, b. 1944) — calligraphy
- Mohamed Melehi (Morocco, b. 1936) — painting
- Rachid Koraichi (Algeria, b. 1947) — installation, calligraphy
- El-Salahi (Sudan, b. 1930) — painting, drawing

### Arab Surrealism
- Ramses Younan (Egypt, 1913–1966)
- Georges Schéhadé (Lebanon)
- Contemporary: Kader Attia (Algeria/France)

### Diaspora Artists
- Mona Hatoum (Palestine/Lebanon/UK, b. 1952) — installation, sculpture
- Shirin Neshat (Iran/US, b. 1957) — photography, video
- Lalla Essaydi (Morocco/US, b. 1956) — photography
- Ghada Amer (Egypt/US, b. 1963) — painting, sculpture
