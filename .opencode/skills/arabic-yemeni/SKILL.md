# arabic-yemeni — Yemeni Arabic

## Overview

Yemeni Arabic (اللهجة اليمانية) is arguably the most linguistically conservative and diverse Arabic dialect cluster. Due to Yemen's mountainous terrain and historical isolation, the peninsula's pre-Islamic linguistic diversity is still visible in modern speech. It preserves:

1. **Archaic Arabic features**: phonemes and grammatical structures from Old Arabic spoken in pre-Islamic Arabia
2. **South Arabian substrate**: the indigenous languages of pre-Islamic Yemen (Sabaean, Minaean, Qatabanian, Hadramite) and their modern descendants (Mehri, Soqotri, Shehri, Bathari, Hobyot)
3. **Trade adstrates**: Indian Ocean trade brought lexical influence from Gujarati, Swahili, Persian, Turkish, Portuguese, and English

## Regional Variations

### Sana'ani (Northern Highlands)

The prestige dialect of Yemen's capital region. Features:

- **Qaf → Qaf** preserved: one of few Arabic dialects that maintains ق as a deep uvular stop
- **Jīm → Jīm** (ج preserved as /dʒ/): جميل → jamīl (same as MSA)
- **Distinctive stress**: stress falls on the penultimate syllable more consistently than other dialects
- **Glottal stop preservation**: hamza is very strong (distinct from dialects that weaken hamza)
- **Vocabulary**: extensive South Arabian (Himyaritic) substrate
- **Politeness particles**: heavy use of دَه (dah, please) and زِين (zēn, good)

### Adenese / Tihami (Southern Coastal)

- **Qaf → Qaf** (preserved in conservative speech) or **Qaf → Glottal** (urban Aden)
- **Indian Ocean vocabulary**: بَزَر (bazar, market), مِيري (meri, sea), بَنْدَر (bandar, port), كُوم (kūm, ship)
- **Coffee trade vocabulary**: uniquely preserved from Mocha/Mokha (المُخا) heritage
- **Swahili and Gujarati**: some loanwords from centuries of Indian Ocean navigators
- **Phonology**: more relaxed emphasis than the highlands

### Hadrami (Eastern Yemen)

- **Conservative Bedouin phonology**: heavy, guttural, maintains many classical features
- **Diphthong preservation**: bayt (بيت) rather than bēt, yawm (يوم) rather than yōm
- **Conditionals**: unique system with لا- (lā) and دَه- (dah-) particles
- **Indian Ocean**: stronger Gujarati and Malay influence due to Hadrami diaspora in Indonesia, Malaysia, Singapore
- **Hadhrami diaspora**: large community in Saudi Arabia, Indonesia, East Africa; distinctive lexical features carried

### Mahri / Soqotri (Modern South Arabian)

**These are NOT Arabic dialects** — they are separate Central Semitic languages spoken in Yemen:

- **Mehri**: spoken in eastern Yemen (al-Mahra governorate) and Oman; ~200,000 speakers
- **Soqotri**: spoken on Socotra Island; ~100,000 speakers; extremely isolated and endangered
- **Shehri / Jibbali**: spoken in Dhofar, Oman (crosses from Yemen border)
- **Features**: non-Arabic phonology (lateral fricatives, ejective consonants), completely different verbal system, pre-Islamic vocabulary preserved nowhere else

**Important**: Bilingual Yemenis speak Arabic and their South Arabian mother tongue. Do not classify or frame South Arabian languages as "dialects of Arabic."

## Core Grammar

### Verb System

| Tense | Sana'ani | Adenese |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Past | Perfective stem | Perfective stem |
| Present | **bi-** prefix (بِيشوف) | **bi-** or **ya-** prefix |
| Future | **ʿalā mā / ʿalā + verb** (على ما يشوف) | **raḥ- or **ʿalā**** |
| Progressive | **qāʿid + verb** (قاعد يشوف) | **qāʿid** (same) |
| Negated past | **mā + verb + -sh** OR **mā + verb** | **mā + verb** |

Distinctive: The conditional and relative clause structures in Yemeni are radically different from other dialects, using:
- **لا (lā)** as a conditional marker in Hadrami
- **دَه (dah)** / **دِي (dī)** as relative pronouns (equivalent to الذي/التي in MSA)

### Possession

Yemeni possession uses a distinctive system:

| Person | Sana'ani | Meaning |
|--------|----------|---------|
| My | -ī / -yā | |
| Your (m) | -ak / -ka / -k | |
| Your (f) | -ish / -ki | |
| His | -ah / -hu | |
| Her | -hā / -hā | |
| Our | -nā / -nā | |
| Your (pl) | -kum / -kin (f) | |
| Their | -hum / -hin (f) | |

Independent possessive: **بُوع (būʿ)** + suffix: بُوعي (būʿī — mine)

## Cultural Context

### Social Structure & Language

- **Qabili (tribal) identity**: dominant in the North; language varies significantly by tribe
- **Sadah (Prophet's descendants)**: highest social status; distinctive speech markers
- **Urban (Aden, Ta'izz, Ibb)**: more "standardized" Yemeni; less tribal vocabulary
- **Gender segregation**: clearly reflected in language — different vocabulary sets for men and women in many areas
- **Coffee & Qat culture** (Mocha coffee heritage vs. contemporary qat chewing): lexical fields strongly developed

### Taboo Topics

- The current civil war (2014–present): Houthi forces (Ansar Allah), the Saudi/UAE-led coalition, the internationally recognized government, the Southern Transitional Council — extremely complex
- **Even naming the warring factions can be read as taking sides**
- Do not simplify or editorialize the conflict
- Zaydi Shia vs Shafi'i Sunni identity — the civil war has sectarian dimensions; avoid oversimplification
- Southern secession / Al-Hirak — extremely sensitive
- Qatar-Gulf rift, Yemen's relationship with Saudi Arabia, Iran's role — all sensitive
- **Landmines**: Yemen has one of the world's worst landmine problems; avoid war metaphors that trivialize
- The "Arabia Felix" (Happy Arabia) framing: beautiful but can read as Orientalist
