# arabic-maghrebi — Maghrebi Arabic (Darija)

## Overview

Maghrebi Arabic (الدارجة / الدارجة المغاربية) is the westernmost branch of Arabic, spoken from Western Sahara/Mauritania to western Egypt. It is the dialect group **least mutually intelligible** with Mashreqi (Eastern) Arabic due to:

1. **Berber (Amazigh) substrate** — phonology, grammar, and extensive vocabulary from Tamazight languages
2. **French superstrate** — especially in Morocco, Algeria, and Tunisia, where French is embedded in everyday language
3. **Short-vowel deletion** — creates consonant clusters that sound radically different to Eastern Arabic speakers
4. **Distinctive negation patterns** — triple negation (mā-...-š + hatta) unique to Maghreb

## National Distinctions

### Moroccan Darija

| Feature | Casablanca/Rabat (Standard) | Fes/Meknes (Northern) | Marrakech/Agadir (South) |
|---------|---------------------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Qaf → q | Qaf as q | q | q with Berber accent |
| Short vowels | Heavily deleted | Less deletion | Moderate |
| Berber influence | Moderate | Moderate | Heavy (Tashelhit area) |
| Future prefix | غَ (gha-) / مَاشِي (māshī) | غَ (gha-) | غَادي (ghādī) |

Distinctive: tripartite negation **mā‑...‑š** → but with **hadd / ḥətta** for absolutes: مَا قُلْتُ حَتَّى شَيْ (ma qult ḥatta shay — I said nothing at all)

### Algerian Darija

| Region | Features |
|--------|----------|
| Algiers | Heavy French code-switching; distinctive trilled r; q → q preserved |
| Oran | Shares features with Eastern Moroccan; Spanish loanwords (due to proximity) |
| Constantine | Conservative; less French, more Berber; q → g in many lexemes |
| Sahara (Ghardaia, Tamanrasset) | Isolated dialects with significant Berber and Tuareg influence |

Suffix conjugation is distinctive:
- "I went" → **رحت (rəḥt)** in most → **مشيت (mšīt)** in Algiers
- Future prefix: **رَاح (rāḥ)** in most → **بـ (b-)** in conservative areas

### Tunisian Darija

| Feature | Tunis | Sahel (Sousse, Sfax) | South |
|---------|-------|---------------------|-------|
| Qaf | q → glottal or preserved | q → glottal | q preserved |
| French vocabulary | Very high | High | Moderate |
| Berber vocabulary | Minimal | Moderate | Higher |

Tunisian distinctive features:
- Future: **باش (bāsh)** + verb
- Question marker: **هَلْـ / هَلِّـ (hal- / halli‑)** attached to verbs
- Diminutives: very productive and noticeable (kətba → ktība)

### Libyan Arabic

| Region | Features |
|--------|----------|
| Tripoli (Western) | Similar to Tunisian; some Italian loanwords |
| Benghazi (Eastern) | Shares more with Egyptian/Levantine; q → g |
| Fezzan (Southern) | Bedouin/Tuareg influence; very conservative |

Libyan distinctive: **well-preserved classical Arabic archaisms** alongside Italian loanwords (furnett, benzina, salata).

### Hassaniya Arabic

Spoken in Mauritania, Western Sahara, southern Morocco, northern Senegal/Mali:

- Extremely **conservative phonology**: preserves q (ق), ḍ (ض), ẓ (ظ) distinctly — rare in other Maghrebi dialects
- Future: **la- / lā- prefix** — unique across Arabic dialects
- Very high Arabic purity; minimal Berber/French but some Wolof/Soninke from West Africa
- Male/female speech differences more pronounced than in other dialects

## Core Grammar

### Phonological Distinctives (vs Eastern Arabic)

| Feature | Maghrebi | Eastern Arabic | Example |
|---------|----------|----------------|---------|
| Short vowels | Deleted or centralized | Preserved | كتب → ktəb vs katab |
| Final -a | -a or -ah preserved | -a / -i | |
| Diphthongs | ay → ī, aw → ū | Often preserved | بيت → bīt vs bayt |
| Qaf | q, g, or glottal | q / g / 2 depending | |
| Jīm | j or zh | j / g depending | |

### Verb System

| Tense | Maghrebi Pattern | Example (Moroccan) |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|
| Past | Perfective stem | كتبت (ktəbt) — I wrote |
| Present | **ka-/ta-** + prefix | كَنكتب (kanəktəb) — I write |
| Future | **gha-/bāsh-/rāḥ-** + prefix | غَنكتب (ghanktəb) — I will write |
| Progressive | **ʿammāl / tā-** | عَمَّال يكتب — he is writing |

### Negation

Maghrebi is famous for **tripartite** and **circumfix** negation:

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Simple verb | mā-...-š | مَا كْتَبْش (ma ktəbš) |
| With absolutes | mā-...-ḥəttā | مَا كْتَبْ حَتَّى كْلْمَة (ma ktəb ḥətta klma) |
| Non-verbal | mā-...-š (same) | مَاشِي (māšī or maši) |
| Future negative | mā-gha-...-š | مَا غَنَكْتَبْش (ma ghanktəbš) |

### Question Words

| English | Moroccan | Algerian | Tunisian | Libyan |
|---------|----------|----------|----------|--------|
| What? | شنو (shnu) / آش (ash) | واش (wāsh) | شنو (shnū) / آش (āsh) | شني (shnī) |
| Why? | علاش (ʿlash) | علاه (ʿlāh) | علاش (ʿlēsh) | علاش (ʿlāsh) |

| Where? | فين (fīn) | وين (wīn) | وين (wīn) | وين (wēn) |

## Cultural Context

### French Code-Switching Norms

In Maghrebi Darija, French is not a foreign language — it is an integral part of the register:

| Domain | French used naturally | Arabic used naturally |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Technology | This is always French | Never Arabicized |
| Education | Technical/academic terms always French | Home/family topics Arabic |
| Administration | Government forms and bureaucracy in French | Traditional crafts Arabic |
| Medicine | All medical terms French | Folk remedies Arabic |

**Rule for content creation:** In Maghrebi Darija, French terms for modern/conceptual vocabulary read as native; Arabicized alternatives read as forced or artificial.

### Berber Identity & Language

- Tamazight is an official language in Morocco and Algeria (since 2011/2016)
- Amazigh (Berber) identity is strong, especially in:
  - Morocco: Rif (Tarifit), Atlas/Tashelhit, Central (Tamazight)
  - Algeria: Kabyle (Taqbaylit), Chaoui, Mozabite, Tuareg
  - Tunisia: limited Berber (Djerba, Matmata)
- Do not conflate "Arab" and "Amazigh" — many Maghrebis identify primarily as Amazigh
- Tifinagh script is used in public signage in Morocco alongside Arabic and French

### Taboo Topics

- Western Sahara conflict — extremely sensitive in Morocco
- Algerian civil war (1991–2002) — still traumatic; do not reference
- Berber/Arab identity tensions — sensitive; do not frame as conflict
- Monarchy (Morocco) — never criticize the King (Amir al-Mu'minin)
- Military (Algeria) — deeply embedded in politics; sensitive
- French colonial legacy — lingering sensitivity across all Maghreb states
- Post-2011 uprisings and their aftermaths — still unfolding
- Quran translations in Darija — controversial (considered by some as disrespect)
