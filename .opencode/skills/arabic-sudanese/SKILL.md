# arabic-sudanese — Sudanese Arabic

## Overview

Sudanese Arabic (اللهجة السودانية / عربي سوداني) is the Arabic dialect continuum of the Nile Valley from the Egyptian border to South Sudan. It is the dominant language of Sudan and is also spoken widely in South Sudan as a lingua franca. Sudanese Arabic is notable for:

1. **Substrate diversity**: Nubian (Nobiin, Kenzi, Midob), Beja (Bedawiyet), Fur, Nilo-Saharan (Dinka, Nuer, Shilluk), and Cushitic languages
2. **Phonological archaisms**: preserves some classical features lost in other dialects alongside innovative diphthongization
3. **The Juba Arabic offshoot**: a stabilized Arabic pidgin that became a creole, spoken by millions in South Sudan

## Regional Variations

### Khartoum Standard (Central)

The prestige dialect, used in national media. Features:

- **Qaf → Gaf** (ق → گ / g): قال → gāl, قلب → galb
- **Jīm → J** (جيم preserved as ج / j): جميل → jamīl
- **Diphthongization**: words ending in CV become CVC: أب → abb, ما → mā but with distinctive tonal length
- **Negation**: **mā + verb + sh**: مَا قَالْش (mā gālsh), with frequent omission of -sh in affirmative-negative pairs
- **Greetings**: lengthy ritual sequence that can last minutes

### Northern (Ja'ali / Shaygi)

- **Nubian substrate**: heavy; kinship terms, food vocabulary, agricultural terms from Nobiin
- **Qaf → G**: even stronger than Khartoum standard
- **Kaf → Ch** in some contexts: كلب → chalb (dog)
- Distinctive intonation: sing-song prosody compared to Khartoum

### Western (Darfur)

- **Fur, Masalit, Zaghawa substrate**: significant lexical borrowing
- **Phonology**: marked emphasis, heavier glottalization
- **Grammatical gender**: some confusion/distinctive assignment compared to Khartoum norm
- **Vocabulary**: unique words for camel types, desert terrain, tribal structures

### Eastern (Beja-area, Kassala, Port Sudan)

- **Beja (Bedawiyet) substrate**: greetings, pastoral vocabulary, kinship
- **Influence of Yemeni/Hijazi Arabic** via Red Sea trade
- **Pronunciation**: marked by stronger emphasis and gutturals
- **Vocabulary**: sea/trade vocabulary from Italian and Turkish via Ottoman Sudan

### Juba Arabic (South Sudan)

Juba Arabic is a stabilized Arabic-based pidgin/creole, distinct from all other Arabic varieties:

| Feature | Juba Arabic | Khartoum Arabic |
|---------|------------|-----------------|
| Gender | None (one pronoun: hu) | Masculine/feminine |
| Tense marking | Pre-verbal particles (gí-, -in) | Inflectional |
| Plural | Suffix -át (kitāb → kitābát) | Broken plurals |
| Grammar | Isolating / analytic | Synthetic |
| Lexicon | Simplified, some reanalysis | Full Arabic |
| Negation | má + verb | mā + verb + -sh or mā + verb |

## Core Grammar

### Verb System

| Tense | Marking | Example (Khartoum) |
|-------|---------|--------------------|
| Past | Perfective stem | شفت (shuft) — I saw |
| Present | **bi-** prefix + imperfect | بشوف (bishūf) — I see |
| Future | **ha-** prefix (from rāḥ) | حَشوف (hashūf) — I will see |
| Progressive | **gāʿid / gāʿda** | قاعد بشوف — I am seeing (literally: sitting seeing) |

### Negation

| Tense | Pattern | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Past | mā + verb (+sh optional) | مَا شُفْت (mā shuft) |
| Present | mā + bi- + verb | مَا بِشُوف (mā bishūf) |
| Non-verbal | mā + noun/adjective | مَا زَيْن (mā zēn) |
| Existential | mā fī (ما في) | There is not |

## Cultural Context

### Greeting Ritual

The Sudanese greeting is famously elaborate. A proper greeting may take 1–2 minutes:

1. **السلام عليكم** (Salām ʿalaykum)
2. **وعليكم السلام** (Wa ʿalaykum al-salām)
3. **كيف حالك؟** (Kēf ḥālak?)
4. **الحمد لله، كيف حالك أنت؟** (Al-ḥamdulillāh, kēf ḥālak inta?)
5. **الحمد لله** (Al-ḥamdulillāh)
6. **كم حالك؟** (kān ḥalāk?) — "how are things" (distinctive Sudanese follow-up)
7. **ما في زين** (mā fī zēn) — "everything good" (Sudanese set phrase)
8. Greetings to family: **أهلك كيفين؟** (Ahlak kēfīn? — How's your family?)
9. Slight bow or hand-on-chest gesture (formal)

This is not optional. Shortened greetings read as rude.

### Diversity Awareness

- Sudan is massively diverse: over 500 ethnic groups, 100+ languages
- Arab identity: not all Sudanese identify as Arab; many identify as Nubian, Beja, Nuba, Fur, etc.
- The 2019 revolution and 2023 war are extremely sensitive topics
- Juba Arabic and South Sudan: South Sudan is an independent country since 2011; do not frame as part of Sudan
- Egyptian vs Sudanese relations: complex mix of kinship and competition; avoid generalizations
